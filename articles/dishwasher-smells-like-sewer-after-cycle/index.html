Check the drain hose routing first. Normal vs not normal

When a mild odor is expected and when a sewer smell signals a problem

A faint, slightly sour smell when you first open the door after a cycle is normal in many homes, especially if the dishwasher ran a heated dry and some moisture remained inside. That mild odor dissipates within a few minutes and does not return between cycles.

A strong sewer or rotten-egg smell that hits you as soon as the cycle ends and lingers even after the door has been open for a while is not normal. It means wastewater or sewer gas is entering the tub, usually through improper drain hose routing or a blocked air gap. That smell will not improve with routine filter cleaning alone; you must fix the drain path first.

Start here

Safe checks to eliminate a sewer smell after the cycle

  1. Inspect the drain hose routing. Trace the drain hose from the dishwasher to the sink drain or garbage disposal connection. The hose should rise at least 32 inches above the floor—creating a high-loop—before dropping down to the drain connection. This high-loop prevents wastewater from the sink or disposal from siphoning back into the dishwasher tub. If the hose sits flat or dips below the drain connection, reroute it or install an air gap. See Dishwasher Drains Into Sink When Running for drain routing guidance.
  2. Check the air gap if your installation has one. An air gap is a small fitting mounted on the sink or countertop that creates a physical break between the dishwasher drain hose and the sink drain. If the air gap is clogged with food debris or mineral buildup, water cannot drain properly and sewer gas can flow back. Remove the air gap cover, clean the interior with a soft brush, and flush with warm water. See Dishwasher Air Gap Overflowing for detailed air gap maintenance.
  3. Clean the filter and filter well. Remove the filter assembly, scrub the mesh under warm water with a soft brush, and scrub the filter well cavity with mild dish soap. Food sludge that sits in the filter well for days or weeks rots and produces a sewage-like odor even when the hose routing is correct. See How To Clean Dishwasher Filter for step-by-step instructions.
  4. Wipe the door gasket and lower edge. Mold and trapped food in the bottom corners of the gasket add a musty layer on top of the sewer smell. Clean those crevices with a damp microfiber cloth and soft brush.
  5. Run a vinegar cycle. Place two cups of white vinegar in a bowl on the bottom rack and run the hottest empty cycle. Vinegar dissolves mineral scale and flushes residual odor from passages inside the tub, spray arms, and drain plumbing that you cannot reach by hand.
  6. Leave the door slightly open after cycles. Between washes, crack the door open an inch or two to let residual moisture evaporate. Trapped moisture feeds the bacteria that produce both musty and sewer-like odors.

Common cause pattern

Why dishwashers produce a sewer smell after the cycle finishes

A sewer smell after the cycle almost always traces back to the drain path rather than to anything inside the tub itself. When the drain hose runs flat or dips below the sink drain connection without a proper high-loop, wastewater from the sink or garbage disposal flows back into the dishwasher tub through the hose after each cycle. That returning wastewater carries the same sewer odor you would expect from a drain, and it sits in the bottom of the tub until the next cycle or until you open the door and notice it.

A blocked or missing air gap produces the same effect. The air gap creates a physical air break between the dishwasher drain and the sink drain, preventing both wastewater and sewer gas from traveling back up the hose. If the air gap is clogged, removed, or never installed, that protective break disappears and the dishwasher drain hose becomes a direct path for sewer gas to enter the tub.

In some cases, the sewer smell comes from food sludge that has accumulated in the filter well and begun to rot. Rotting food in a dark, wet cavity produces an odor similar to a drain, and the smell intensifies right after a cycle because the wash water stirred up the debris and then left it sitting in standing water at the bottom of the well.

Branch selection

If the sewer smell appears only when the garbage disposal runs

When the smell coincides with running the disposal, the drain hose is likely connected directly to the disposal without a high-loop or air gap, and disposal wastewater is pushing back up into the dishwasher. Fix the hose routing first by adding a high-loop or installing an air gap, then clean the filter and run a vinegar cycle to flush the residual waste that already entered the tub.

Branch selection

If the sewer smell persists after fixing the drain hose

When you have confirmed proper hose routing or a functioning air gap and the smell still returns, the source is inside the machine. Clean the filter well thoroughly—food sludge in that cavity can rot independently of drain routing. Also check whether the drain hose has an interior buildup of grease or sludge that prevents complete drainage and leaves enough standing water for odor bacteria to grow. If the filter is clean, the hose is routed correctly, and the smell still persists after a vinegar cycle, the drain pump or internal plumbing may need professional inspection.

Avoidable issues

Mistakes that make a sewer smell harder to eliminate

  • Masking the smell with fragrance tablets or rinse additives instead of fixing the drain routing. Fragrance covers the odor temporarily but does not stop wastewater from siphoning back.
  • Running chemical drain cleaners through the dishwasher. These products are not designed for dishwasher internals and can damage the drain pump, hose, and gasket.
  • Cleaning the filter without also scrubbing the filter well cavity. Sludge in the well continues to rot even when the filter mesh looks clean.
  • Assuming the smell is normal for an older machine. Sewer gas entering the tub is never normal and indicates a drain routing or plumbing issue that should be corrected.
  • Replacing the drain pump before verifying hose routing and air gap condition. Most sewer smells trace back to the hose path, not to pump failure.

Know when to stop

When the sewer smell requires professional service

  • The smell persists after correcting drain hose routing, cleaning the air gap, scrubbing the filter and filter well, and running a full vinegar cycle.
  • The drain hose needs rerouting or replacement that requires plumbing modification beyond a simple high-loop adjustment.
  • An air gap needs to be installed where none currently exists, which may require a new sink fitting or countertop hole.
  • The dishwasher throws drain error codes, does not complete cycles, or leaks water onto the floor.

At that point, a plumber or appliance technician can inspect the full drain path, install or replace an air gap, reroute the hose properly, and test the drain pump for mechanical issues.

Common questions

FAQ

  • Why does my dishwasher smell like a sewer right after the cycle finishes? The most common cause is wastewater siphoning back into the tub through a drain hose that sits too low or lacks a proper high-loop. Food sludge rotting inside the filter well or a blocked air gap can also produce that smell.
  • How do I stop wastewater from siphoning back? Make sure the drain hose has a high-loop that rises at least 32 inches above the floor before dropping to the drain connection, or confirm that your air gap is clear and functioning.
  • Is a sewer smell from the dishwasher dangerous? The smell itself is unpleasant but not typically hazardous at dishwasher scale. However, it indicates that wastewater or sewer gas is entering your appliance, which can contaminate dishes and should be corrected promptly.
  • Can a clogged filter cause a sewer smell? Yes. Rotting food sludge in the filter or filter well produces an odor similar to a drain, especially right after a cycle when the wash water has stirred the debris.
  • Should I run a vinegar cycle every time I notice the smell? Vinegar helps flush residual odor, but it does not fix improper drain routing. Correct the hose high-loop or air gap first, then run vinegar to clear what remains.
  • When should I call a plumber instead of continuing to troubleshoot? Call when the drain hose needs rerouting, the air gap needs installation or replacement, or when the smell persists after all safe maintenance steps have been completed.

Fact check

References and fact-check notes

  • Verify drain hose routing recommendations in your dishwasher's installation guide and local plumbing codes. Some jurisdictions require an air gap rather than a high-loop.
  • Confirm air gap cleaning and removal steps with your specific model's manual before disassembling any drain fittings.
  • Cross-check filter type and removal method with the owner manual. Some filters have a two-piece assembly that must be separated for full cleaning.
  • Keep pump replacement, hose replacement requiring plumbing modification, and air gap installation outside this maintenance-first guide.
