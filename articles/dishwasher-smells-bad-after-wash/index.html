Dishwasher Smells Bad After Wash? 6 Odor Fixes That Actually Work

If your dishwasher smells bad after a wash cycle — especially a wet-dog, musty, rotten-egg, or sewer-like odor — do not just run another cycle and hope for the best. The smell is a clue. This guide walks through six targeted fixes that address the actual source, not just the symptom.

Fast answer

Quick answer

The most common cause of a dishwasher that smells bad after washing is a dirty filter trapping decomposing food particles. The second most common is a drain hose with standing water that has gone stagnant. Start with a filter clean and a hot empty cycle with white vinegar or dishwasher cleaner before investigating deeper.

Smell diagnosis

What the smell type tells you about the problem

Different odors point to different sources. Identifying the specific smell saves you from chasing the wrong fix:

Rotten egg or sewer smell

Standing water somewhere in the drain path has gone stagnant and is producing hydrogen sulfide. Check for water in the sump, a low spot in the drain hose, or a blocked air gap. This is often a drainage problem, not a cleaning problem. See our guide on sewer smells after the cycle for a dedicated walkthrough.

Musty or mildew smell

A moist, sealed environment with food residue encourages mold and bacteria growth. The filter, door gasket folds, and spray arm interiors are the prime suspects. A deep cleaning cycle with hot water and dishwasher cleaner usually resolves this.

Wet dog or damp earth smell

Typically biofilm — a slimy bacterial layer that builds up on the filter, sump walls, and spray arm interiors. This is a cleaning issue. Scrub all accessible surfaces, then run a sanitize cycle if your model has one.

Burnt plastic or electrical smell

Stop using the dishwasher immediately. A burning smell can indicate a failing heating element, a motor bearing seizing, or a wire shorting. This is not a cleaning issue — it is a safety issue. Unplug or turn off the breaker and call a technician.

Start here

6 odor fixes — do them in this order

  1. Remove and deep-clean the filter. Take the filter out entirely (most twist out counterclockwise). Rinse it under hot running water while scrubbing with a soft brush — an old toothbrush works perfectly. Food particles, grease, and biofilm lodge in the mesh. If the water running through the filter looks brown, you have found at least part of the smell. Do not just rinse it — scrub it. See our full filter cleaning guide for step-by-step details.
  2. Wipe out the filter well and sump area. With the filter removed, use paper towels to wipe out the cavity below. You may find sludge, food scraps, glass shards, or labels that have accumulated over months. A flashlight helps here — the sump is dark and easy to miss debris in. Remove everything you can see and reach.
  3. Clean the door gasket and its folds. Open the door and run a damp microfiber cloth along the entire rubber gasket, especially the bottom edge where water pools. Pull back the gasket folds gently — food residue and black mold often hide in these creases. If you see black spots, wipe them with a mixture of warm water and a drop of dish soap. Bleach is not recommended as it can degrade the rubber over time.
  4. Check and clean the spray arms. Remove the lower and upper spray arms (most snap off with gentle upward pressure). Hold them under a faucet and check that water flows freely from every hole. Small food particles, seeds, and mineral deposits clog the nozzles. Use a toothpick or paperclip to clear blocked holes. Rinse thoroughly before reattaching. Clogged spray arms not only reduce wash performance — the trapped debris and moisture inside them contribute to odor.
  5. Run a hot empty cycle with white vinegar or dishwasher cleaner. Place a cup of white vinegar in a dishwasher-safe bowl on the top rack, or use a commercial dishwasher cleaning tablet. Run the hottest and longest cycle your machine offers — the "Sanitize" or "Heavy" cycle. The vinegar's acidity dissolves mineral buildup and kills odor-causing bacteria. The hot water flushes loosened debris through the drain path. Do this at least once a month.
  6. Check the drain hose for standing water. If the smell persists after all five steps above, the odor source may be outside the dishwasher itself. A sagging drain hose can hold a small pool of stagnant water that smells when the machine runs and moves air through the hose. Under the sink, look for a low spot or dip in the corrugated drain hose. The hose should have a high loop that stays above the sink drain connection. If it sags, use a zip tie or hose clamp to support it higher. See drain hose symptoms for more detail.

Why it happens

Why your dishwasher smells even after a wash cycle

This is the part that confuses people — the machine just ran with hot water and detergent, so how can it smell? The answer is that a dishwasher wash cycle cleans dishes, but it does not necessarily clean the dishwasher itself. Food particles that wash off plates collect in the filter and sump. Detergent does not sterilize — it emulsifies grease and suspends particles so they can be drained away. If the filter is already full, those particles never leave. They sit in warm, wet conditions — perfect for bacteria — and the next wash cycle simply stirs them up and redistributes the odor.

A second factor is that modern energy-efficient dishwashers use less water per cycle than older models. Less water means less flushing of the internal drain path. Debris that would have been washed completely through a 1990s dishwasher instead accumulates gradually in a 2020s dishwasher. This is why monthly maintenance cleaning is more important on newer machines, not less.

Common mistakes

4 mistakes that make dishwasher odor worse

Running pre-rinse cycles on already-clean dishes. If you rinse dishes until they look clean before loading, the dishwasher detergent has nothing to work on. The enzymes in modern detergent need food soil to activate. Without it, the detergent underperforms and leaves its own residue behind — which can smell.
Using too much detergent. Excess detergent — especially liquid — leaves a film on the dishwasher interior that traps food particles and provides a surface for bacteria to grow. If you see white residue or a slimy film inside the machine, cut the detergent amount in half and run a cleaning cycle.
Closing the door immediately after a cycle. A dishwasher is a sealed, humid box after the cycle ends. Leave the door cracked open for at least 30 minutes after unloading to let the interior dry. This alone can prevent musty odors from developing between runs.
Ignoring the air gap or disposal connection. If your kitchen sink has an air gap (the chrome cylinder next to the faucet), odor can travel from the sink drain into the dishwasher through a shared drain path. A dirty disposal can also contribute. Run the disposal with cold water for 30 seconds before starting the dishwasher.

Know when to stop

When the smell means it is time for professional service

  • You smell burning plastic or an electrical odor — stop using the machine and call a technician immediately.
  • You have completed all six fixes and the odor returns within one or two cycles.
  • The smell is accompanied by water on the floor, tripping breakers, or error codes.
  • You see black mold growth that extends beyond what you can reach with household cleaning — this may require partial disassembly.

Questions readers ask

FAQ

  • Why does my dishwasher smell like rotten eggs only when it runs? The pump and spray arms circulate air through the drain path during operation, pushing stagnant-water odors into the kitchen. The smell source is usually standing water in the sump, drain hose, or air gap. Start with the filter-and-sump cleaning described above.
  • Can I use bleach to clean a smelly dishwasher? It is not recommended. Bleach is harsh on rubber gaskets, can corrode metal components, and may react with any detergent residue to produce harmful fumes. White vinegar or a commercial dishwasher cleaner is safer and equally effective for odor removal.
  • How often should I clean the dishwasher filter to prevent odors? Monthly for a household that runs the dishwasher 4+ times per week. Every two months for lighter use. If you have hard water or cook with a lot of oil and grease, monthly is the safer bet.
  • Why does my dishwasher still smell after I cleaned the filter? The filter is the most common source but not the only one. Move next to the door gasket folds, spray arm interiors, and the drain hose routing under the sink. Also check whether the odor is worse when the dishwasher has not been used for a few days — that points toward stagnant water rather than surface residue.
  • Can old dishes or a dirty kitchen sink cause the dishwasher to smell? Yes. If the dishwasher drain hose connects to the sink drain or disposal, odor from a dirty sink trap or disposal can travel up the hose when the dishwasher is not running. This is especially common if the high loop in the drain hose has fallen below the sink drain level.

References

Fact-check notes

  • Biofilm formation: Studies on household appliance hygiene confirm that warm, moist environments with organic matter support bacterial biofilm within 24–48 hours. This is the mechanism behind the "wet dog" smell in dishwashers.
  • Vinegar efficacy: Acetic acid (5% concentration in household white vinegar) is effective against common household bacteria and mold at contact times of 10+ minutes. Hot wash cycles provide sufficient temperature and duration for this to work.
  • Drain hose high loop: Plumbing codes (IPC Section 802.1.6) require dishwasher drain lines to be fastened as high as possible under the countertop to prevent backflow. A fallen high loop is a code violation as well as an odor source.
  • Detergent residue: Modern enzyme-based detergents (protease, amylase) require food soil as a substrate. Pre-rinsing dishes reduces detergent effectiveness and leaves unspent detergent residue — a known contributor to dishwasher odor and film buildup.
